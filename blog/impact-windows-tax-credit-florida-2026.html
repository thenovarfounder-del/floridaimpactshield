Impact Windows Tax Credit Florida 2026: Complete Guide to Federal & State Incentives

If you're planning to install impact windows in Florida during 2026, understanding the available tax credits and incentives can save you thousands of dollars. With hurricane season becoming increasingly unpredictable and insurance premiums continuing to rise, many Florida homeowners are turning to impact-resistant windows as both a protective measure and a smart financial investment. The good news is that qualifying impact windows may be eligible for substantial federal tax credits, along with Florida-specific benefits that make this upgrade more affordable than ever.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know about the impact windows tax credit in Florida for 2026, including federal incentives, state exemptions, financing options, and strategies for maximizing your tax benefits when you install these essential home improvements.

Federal Energy Tax Credit: Section 25C Explained

The federal government's Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit, commonly known as Section 25C, has been extended and enhanced through recent legislation, making 2026 an excellent year to invest in qualifying impact windows. This tax credit is designed to encourage homeowners to make energy-efficient upgrades that reduce their home's overall energy consumption while providing additional benefits like storm protection.

Under the current Section 25C provisions, homeowners can claim a tax credit equal to 30% of the cost of qualifying impact windows, up to a maximum credit of $600 per year. This means if you spend $2,000 or more on eligible impact windows with energy-efficient coatings, you could receive the maximum $600 credit when you file your 2026 tax return.

Important Note: The $600 limit applies specifically to windows and skylights. Other energy-efficient home improvements have separate limits under Section 25C, with an overall annual credit limit of $3,200 for all qualifying improvements combined.

What makes this particularly attractive for Florida homeowners is that impact windows serve a dual purpose: they protect your home from hurricane damage while also reducing your cooling costs through improved insulation and UV protection. When you choose impact windows with Low-E coatings, you're getting both safety and energy efficiency in one investment.

Which Impact Windows Qualify for the Federal Tax Credit?

Not all impact windows automatically qualify for the federal tax credit. To be eligible, your impact windows must meet specific ENERGY STAR requirements for energy efficiency. Here's what you need to know:

ENERGY STAR Certification Requirements

For impact windows to qualify for the Section 25C tax credit, they must be ENERGY STAR certified and meet the program's strict performance standards for the Southern climate zone, which includes all of Florida. These requirements include:

  • U-Factor rating of 0.40 or lower (measures how well the window insulates)
  • Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) of 0.25 or lower (measures how well the window blocks heat from sunlight)
  • Must be certified by the manufacturer as meeting ENERGY STAR Most Efficient criteria
  • Must include Low-E (low-emissivity) coating to reflect infrared light

The Low-E coating requirement is particularly important for Florida homeowners. This microscopically thin metallic coating reflects heat while allowing visible light to pass through, significantly reducing your air conditioning costs during Florida's long, hot summers. Most quality impact windows designed for Florida's climate already include Low-E coatings, but you should verify this with your installer before purchase.

Documentation from Your Impact Window Manufacturer

When you purchase qualifying impact windows, the manufacturer must provide a written certification statement confirming that the products meet the ENERGY STAR requirements. This certification should include:

  • Manufacturer name and product model number
  • U-Factor and SHGC ratings
  • Confirmation of ENERGY STAR certification
  • Statement that the products meet Section 25C requirements

Keep this certification with your tax records, as you may need it if the IRS requests documentation to support your tax credit claim.

How to Claim the Impact Windows Tax Credit: IRS Form 5695

Claiming your impact windows tax credit is a straightforward process that you'll complete when filing your 2026 federal tax return in early 2027. Here's the step-by-step process:

First, you'll need to complete IRS Form 5695, titled "Residential Energy Credits." This is the same form used for solar panels and other energy-efficient home improvements. For impact windows, you'll focus on Part II of the form, which covers the Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit.

Step-by-Step Filing Instructions

  • Line 14a: Enter the total cost of your qualifying impact windows, including installation costs
  • Calculate the credit: The form will automatically calculate 30% of your costs
  • Apply the $600 cap: Even if 30% of your costs exceeds $600, your credit for windows is limited to this amount
  • Transfer to Form 1040: The final credit amount from Form 5695 transfers to Schedule 3 of your Form 1040

Tax Filing Tip: The Section 25C credit is non-refundable, meaning it can reduce your tax liability to zero but won't generate a refund if the credit exceeds your taxes owed. However, unlike previous versions of this credit, any unused portion may be carried forward to future tax years under current provisions.

Florida State Incentives for Impact Windows

While Florida doesn't have state income tax, which means there's no state-level income tax credit for impact windows, the Sunshine State does offer valuable sales tax benefits that can significantly reduce your upfront costs.

Florida Sales Tax Exemption for Impact-Resistant Products

Florida offers a sales tax exemption during designated disaster preparedness periods, typically held before hurricane season begins. During these tax holidays, impact-resistant windows and doors are exempt from the state's 6% sales tax (plus any applicable local option taxes).

For 2026, watch for announcements about the disaster preparedness sales tax holiday, which has historically occurred in late May or early June. During this period, which typically lasts one to two weeks, you can purchase impact windows without paying sales tax, regardless of the purchase price. On a $10,000 impact window installation, this exemption could save you $600 or more in sales taxes.

However, it's important to note that even outside these designated periods, some jurisdictions may offer year-round exemptions for hurricane-protection products. Check with your local tax authority or impact window installer to confirm current exemptions in your area.

PACE Financing and Tax Deductions

Property Assessed Clean Energy (PACE) financing has become an increasingly popular option for Florida homeowners installing impact windows. This unique financing mechanism allows you to pay for energy-efficient and storm-resilient home improvements through an assessment on your property tax bill.

From a tax perspective, PACE financing offers potential benefits. The interest portion of your PACE payments may be tax-deductible as home mortgage interest, subject to IRS limits and requirements. However, the tax treatment of PACE financing can be complex, and the IRS has issued limited guidance on this topic.

To qualify for the interest deduction, the PACE assessment generally must be secured by your home and used for improvements that substantially improve your property. Most impact window installations meet these criteria. However, you should consult with a tax professional to ensure you're properly claiming this deduction and maintaining adequate documentation.

My Safe Florida Home Grant: Tax Implications

The My Safe Florida Home program provides matching grants to eligible Florida homeowners for hurricane-resistant improvements, including impact windows. If you receive a grant through this program in 2026, you need to understand the tax implications.

Generally, the My Safe Florida Home grant is not considered taxable income for federal or state tax purposes because it's a qualified disaster mitigation payment. However, receiving this grant affects how you calculate your federal tax credit:

  • You must subtract the grant amount from your total costs before calculating the 30% tax credit
  • For example, if your impact windows cost $5,000 and you receive a $2,500 grant, you calculate the tax credit on $2,500 ($5,000 - $2,500)
  • The maximum $600 credit still applies to your reduced cost basis

This prevents "double-dipping" by ensuring you don't receive both grant funding and a tax credit on the same dollars spent. For detailed information about grant eligibility and application procedures, visit our My Safe Florida Home grants page.

Impact Windows as Rental Property Deductions

If you're installing impact windows on a Florida rental property, the tax treatment differs significantly from the homeowner tax credit. As a rental property owner, you cannot claim the Section 25C tax credit because this credit is exclusively for improvements to your principal residence.

However, you may actually receive better tax benefits through business expense deductions:

Depreciation Deduction

Impact windows installed on rental property are considered improvements to the building and can be depreciated over 27.5 years for residential rental properties. This means you can deduct a portion of the installation cost each year as a depreciation expense against your rental income.

Section 179 Deduction

Depending on your specific situation, you may be able to immediately expense a portion of your impact window costs under Section 179, rather than depreciating them over time. This provision allows certain business property improvements to be fully deducted in the year of installation, subject to annual limits.

Maintenance and Repair Deductions

If you're replacing existing windows rather than installing impact windows for the first time, portions of the project might qualify as repairs rather than improvements, potentially allowing for immediate deduction rather than depreciation.

The tax treatment of rental property improvements is complex and highly dependent on your specific circumstances. Working with a CPA who understands real estate taxation is essential for maximizing your deductions while maintaining IRS compliance.

Documenting Your Impact Window Purchase for Tax Purposes

Proper documentation is crucial for claiming your impact windows tax credit and withstanding potential IRS scrutiny. Here's what you should keep in your tax records:

Essential Documentation Checklist

  • Itemized invoice or receipt: Showing the total cost of materials and installation, dated in 2026
  • Manufacturer's certification: Confirming ENERGY STAR compliance and energy performance ratings
  • Product specifications: Including model numbers, U-Factor, and SHGC ratings
  • Installation contract: Detailing the scope of work and property address
  • Proof of payment: Canceled checks, credit card statements, or loan documents
  • Before and after photos: Though not required, these can help document the improvement
  • Building permits: If required by your local jurisdiction

Store these documents for at least seven years, which is beyond the typical three-year IRS audit period but provides protection in case of extended scrutiny. Digital copies stored securely in the cloud provide additional backup protection.

Pro Tip: Create a dedicated folder in your tax records labeled "2026 Impact Windows Tax Credit" and store all related documents together. This organization will make tax filing much easier and ensure you have everything readily available if questions arise.

Working with a CPA for Maximum Tax Benefits

While many homeowners successfully claim the impact windows tax credit on their own, working with a qualified CPA or tax professional can help ensure you're maximizing all available benefits while maintaining compliance with IRS requirements.

A tax professional can help you:

  • Determine if you qualify for additional energy efficiency credits for other improvements made in the same year
  • Optimize the timing of your purchase if you're considering multiple tax years
  • Navigate complex situations like partial business use of your home or multi-unit properties
  • Coordinate federal credits with grant programs to avoid reporting errors
  • Plan for PACE financing interest deductions
  • Ensure proper documentation and record retention

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I claim the federal tax credit for impact windows installed in 2025 when I file my 2026 tax return?

No, you can only claim the tax credit for the year in which the impact windows were installed and paid for. If your windows were installed in 2025, you would claim the credit on your 2025 tax return filed in early 2026. The credit must be claimed in the tax year the expense was incurred.

Do impact windows installed on a second home in Florida qualify for the tax credit?

Yes, the Section 25C tax credit can be claimed for qualifying energy-efficient improvements to a second home that you own and use as a residence, as long as you don't rent it out. However, you cannot claim the credit for rental properties or homes you don't use personally.

If my impact windows cost $1,500, will I get a $450 tax credit (30% of $1,500)?

Yes, if your qualifying impact windows cost $1,500, your tax credit would be $450 (30% of $1,500), which is under the $600 maximum for windows. However, this assumes your windows meet all ENERGY STAR requirements and include Low-E coatings. The credit is calculated on your total qualifying costs including professional installation.

Can I claim both the federal tax credit and the My Safe Florida Home grant for the same windows?

Yes, you can receive both benefits, but you must reduce your cost basis for the tax credit calculation by the amount of the grant received. You cannot claim the 30% tax credit on the portion of costs covered by the grant. This prevents receiving duplicate tax benefits on the same expense.

What happens if my tax credit exceeds my tax liability for 2026?

The Section 25C tax credit is non-refundable, meaning it can only reduce your tax liability to zero—it won't generate a refund. However, under current law, if your credit exceeds your tax liability, you may be able to carry the unused portion forward to future tax years. Consult with a tax professional about carryforward provisions specific to your situation.
